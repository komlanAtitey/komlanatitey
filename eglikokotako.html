Bio


Dr. Komlan Atitey is a Research Fellow in the Biostatistics and Computational Biology Branch at the National Institute of Environmental Health Sciences (NIEHS), where he specializes in high-dimensional data analysis, cancer evolution modeling, and AI-driven biological inference. His work bridges machine learning, mechanistic and probabilistic modeling, and systems biology to unravel the mechanisms underlying tumor progression, gene-gene interactions, and environmental influences on disease. He has developed multiple impactful computational tools, including MMQE, DEGBOE, MIBCOVIS, GIBOOST, CancerTrace, and magicSubclonal, a physics-informed AI framework that integrates chemical master equation dynamics to detect driver-gene subclones and assign risk labels. Through these contributions, Dr. Atitey continues to advance single-cell analytics, network inference, and precision medicine.


He received his PhD in Computational Biology from the College of Engineering at Swansea University, United Kingdom, in August 2019. Prior to that, he earned a bachelor’s and a master’s degree in Physics from the Faculty of Sciences at the University of Lomé, Togo, in 2006 and 2007, respectively, and a second master’s degree in Information and Communication Engineering from Harbin Engineering University in Harbin, Heilongjiang, China, in 2015. In 2023, he further expanded his expertise by completing two certifications from online courses offered by Harvard University in Massachusetts, United States: one in Data Science - Machine Learning, and another in High-Dimensional Data Analysis.


Before joining NIEHS in November 2020, Dr. Atitey served as a Postdoctoral Research Associate at the University of Nebraska-Lincoln in Lincoln, NE, USA, where he worked on a Multiscale Multicellular Mathematical Model of Immunogenicity. Earlier in his career, following his first master’s degree in Physics, he spent five years teaching high school Physics in Togo.

Publications


  • “CancerTrace: Multi-stage single-cell analysis of networked cancer evolution for driver and modulator gene identification” is out. CancerTrace is a time-aware, single-cell RNA-seq framework that infers patient-specific, causal driver–modulator relationships by reconstructing stage-resolved expression dynamics, enabling directed influence mapping and in-silico perturbation without requiring matched DNA or large cohorts.

  • “Physics-informed AI with chemical master equation dynamics for driver-gene subclone detection and risk labeling” is out. magicSubclonal is a physics-informed framework that models driver-gene burst dynamics to identify maximally separable rare subclones from bulk transcriptomes, linking these states to downstream genes with controlled false discovery and delivering reproducible, clinically predictive subclone labels that outperform existing mixture, clustering, and deconvolution methods.

  • “Boosting data interpretation with GIBOOST to enhance visualization of complex high-dimensional data” is out. GIBOOST integrates multiple high-dimensional single-cell datasets to enhance spatial data visualization and improve interpretability by optimally combining complementary dimensionality reduction methods.

  • “Model-based evaluation of spatiotemporal data reduction methods with unknown ground truth through optimal visualization and interpretability metrics” is out. This work enhances the visualization and interpretability of high-dimensional data without relying on ground truth by integrating five robust metrics, including a novel time-ordered Markov-based structural metric, into a semi-supervised hierarchical Bayesian model.

  • “DEGBOE: Discrete time Evolution modeling of Gene mutation through Bayesian inference using qualitative Observation of mutation Events” is out. This work enhances the understanding of gene-gene interactions in the dynamics of gene mutations that drive cancer development and progression.

  • “Mathematical Modeling of Proliferative Immune Response Initiated by Interactions Between Classical Antigen-Presenting Cells Under Joint Antagonistic IL-2 and IL-4 Signaling” is out. This work investigates the combination of machine learning and mechanistic modeling to comprehend the mechanisms of the dynamic interplay of lymphocyte proliferation with the complex interaction effects of cytokines during an immune response to pathogen invasion.

  • “Comprehensive Review of Models and Methods for Inferences in Bio-Chemical Reaction Networks” is out. This investigation demonstrates that the most common models of biochemical reaction networks involve differential equations, Markov processes, mass action kinetics, and state space representations. Additionally, the most common tasks associated with these models are parameter inference and model identification.

  • “Inferring distributions from observed mRNA and protein copy counts in genetic circuits” is out. This work presents a statistical method to infer distributions of mRNA and protein counts from observed data. The main advantage of this method is that it does not require any prior assumptions or knowledge about the underlying chemical reactions.

  • “Elucidating effects of reaction rates on dynamics of the lac circuit in Escherichia coli” is out. This work quantifies the uncertainty of not knowing the accurate reaction rates of biochemical reactions and aims to understand how these reaction rates affect gene expression.

  • “Determining the Transcription Rates Yielding Steady-State Production of mRNA in the Lac Genetic Switch of Escherichia coli” is out. This work elucidates the regulatory dynamics of gene expression activation and inactivation by evaluating the transcription rates that yield steady-state mRNA production in active and inactive states of the lac circuit, and the results of the studies can be used in synthetic biology applications to understand the limits of genetic synthesis.

  • “A Novel Prediction Algorithm in Gaussian-Mixture Probability Hypothesis Density Filter for Target Tracking” is out. This work provides an improved Gaussian mixture probability hypothesis density filter for multiple target tracking to maintain estimates of the targets' current states.

Computational Tools


  • “CancerTrace”. A time-aware single-cell framework that infers patient-specific cancer driver genes and their modulators, reconstructing stage-resolved regulatory dynamics and causal driver-modulator influence networks.

  • “magicSubclonal”. A framework integrating stochastic driver-gene dynamics and gene expression to resolve rare subclones and assign clinical risk in cancer cohorts. links to GitHub and publication.

  • “GIBOOST”.An AI-driven framework integrating multiple dimensionality reduction methods to enhance clustering sensitivity and preserve biologically meaningful structures in high-dimensional single-cell data.

  • “MIBCOVIS”. A Bayesian framework benchmarking and optimizing high-dimensional data visualization and interpretability, integrating multiple metrics to guide dynamic and spatial single-cell analyses.

  • “MMQE(Multiscale Multicellular Quantitative Evaluator)”. A hybrid computational model capturing cytokine-lymphocyte interactions to predict immune response dynamics.

Manuscript Under Review

Projects 1:


Elucidating the dynamic interplay of tumor heterogeneity and the immune microenvironment during lung cancer progression


Scientific problem and Challenges


  • Cancer is a collection of heterogeneous diseases with varying molecular and phenotypic characteristics ref. The extensive molecular heterogeneity exhibited by tumors complicates the identification of potential cancer drivers. When studying epigenetic events, a potential difficulty, which may also affect cancer metastasis, is the lack of an analytical framework for identifying epigenetic changes in event drivers ref.

  • However, identifying cancer drivers is vital since they directly and/or indirectly provide a selective growth advantage to cancer cells by activating mutations in oncogenes and/or mutations that silence tumor suppressor genes. Nevertheless, investigating interactions between genes associated with cancer is challenging because of the enormous datasets and sophisticated computational analyses required.

    • Proposed solution


    My goal is to develop analytical frameworks that explore the interplay between genetic, epigenetic, and non-genetic mechanisms of cancer to identify cancer drivers for cancer prevention, diagnosis, and treatment.


    atitey

    1. Cancer driver theory and dynamics of tumor heterogeneity


    The DEGBOE computational framework interrogates the dynamic evolution of a gene mutation concerning its interaction with other genes, enabling the exploration of fundamental questions about cancer drivers. Here, on the basis of single cell analysis, I plan to use two approaches to address questions about distinguishing cancer driver mutations from passenger mutations.


    Indeed, single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) is a technology that analyzes the transcriptomes of complex tissues at the single-cell level and reveals the heterogeneity of tumor cells, thus monitoring the progress of tumor development and preventing further cellular deterioration. In this regard, I collected scRNA-seq datasets of lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD) patient-derived cells, covering primary tumor, lymph node, brain metastases, pleural effusion, as well as normal lung tissues and lymph nodes. This enables me to describe the dynamics of LUAD evolution in terms of gene expression in cells in three stages: normal lung, primary LUAD, and metastatic LUAD.


    To identify clusters of each of the data, and relying on the Seurat and tidyverse packages, I performed different steps in scRNA-seq data analysis based on significant principal component. I further used FindAllMarkers() function of Seurat to find markers that define clusters. I began by identifying gene markers of cells in the normal lung data, and for consistency, I used the same gene markers to identify the cell types in the primary and metastatic LUAD data.


    While a single cancer driver is an individual gene which initializes and drives cancer progression, a module of cancer drivers is a set of genes including genes which have a mutual exclusivity of mutations and influence their targets to develop a cancer. I will extend the DEGBOE analytical framework to the extDEGBOE for modeling a number of n cohesive genes which have a high density of interactions in a gene network in driving lung cancer in relation to (N-n) gene-gene interactions and cell-cell interactions.


    The key objective of this project is to develop a computational Markov model of evolutionary dynamics of cancer driver module and identify genetic drivers that silence tumor suppressor genes in the tumor microenvironment to drive cancer growth through interaction between cancer cells and normal cells.

    2. Interplay between genetics and epigenetics mechanisms on the dynamic of cancer drivers


    Epigenetic modifications are primary mechanisms of regulation during carcinogenesis, or cancer progression ref. In addition, the activation of oncogenes and/or suppression of tumor suppressor genes by a cancer driver contributes to the onset of cancer ref.


    I envision the creation of a hybrid model that will accommodate both deterministic and probabilistic information in two complementary ways, which will revolutionize the way we connect carcinogenesis to genetic and epigenetic mechanisms.


  • I will first develop a quantitative deterministic mathematical model (qCancerDr) that describes the dual role of cancer drivers in regulating aspects of cellular metabolism.

  • Second, I will connect epigenetics and genetics mechanisms to study carcinogenesis and cancer progression by linking qCancerDr with extDEGBOE to create a mixed model that identifies cancer drivers in the dynamics of tumor heterogeneity. The mixed model will exploit deterministic information on environmental factors to assess their roles in carcinogenesis.

Projects 2:


Optimizing single-cell spatiotemporal delay variations to identify key features driving progression


Scientific problem and Challenges


    One of the major challenges in modeling the dynamics of a single-cell trajectory using single-cell analysis is dealing with the complexity and variability of biological systems. More so, the high-dimensional nature of single-cell data including the spatial interactions pose a challenge for data visualization, analysis, and interpretation. Therefore, developing and validating accurate and robust models for single-cell spatiotemporal trajectory analysis remains a significant challenge in the field.


    Proposed solution


    Considering the fact that single-cell data are inherently dynamic and stochastic, I propose a multi-state Markov-based computational framework denoted Time Order Structure Learning (TOSL) to tackle the spatiotemporal dependency of ordered cells in the low-dimensional space resulting from temporal data reduction or imaging analysis.


    atitey

    Time Order Structure Learning (TOSL)


    This framework can be adapted to model state transitions in multidimensional data sets with any underlying ordering of data points or trajectory structure. For example, preliminary results suggests that the identification of the variations in delay time to stationarity allows for the identification of therapeutic targets that may be useful to study deviations from normal development resulting in diseases like cancer.


    Optimizing this variability and integrating with feature selection approaches could provide useful insights on the major timepoints and state transitions that drive the underlying biological process.


    My current research in the direction to enhance the TOSL framework to identify genes and cells which trigger the stationarity of state transitions of cells during the developmental biology accounting for gene-gene interaction, and cell-cell communication.


    Primary results of this work has been presented at the 2023 International Conference on Intelligent Systems for Molecular Biology (ISMB 2023) on July 2023 in Lyon, France.
